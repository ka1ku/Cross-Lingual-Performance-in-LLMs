\begin{abstract}
Large language models (LLMs) are trained predominantly on English data yet deployed in
multilingual contexts, raising open questions about how well they generalize across
languages and whether they rely on implicit translation mechanisms when processing
non-English inputs. We study these questions through a two-pronged framework: black-box
accuracy evaluation and white-box mechanistic probing via logit lens and
Representational Similarity Analysis (RSA). We construct a 180-question benchmark
spanning three languages---English, Spanish, and Basque---and three task categories
(math, factual, reasoning), and evaluate two 7B-parameter instruction-tuned models:
Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.2 and Qwen2-7B-Instruct. Mistral achieves 68\% overall
English accuracy but collapses to 8\% on Basque (0\% factual); Qwen2 is significantly
more robust at 37\% Basque overall. Logit lens reveals that math answers are identified
at shallow layers regardless of language, while Basque factual representations become
structurally random by the final layer. RSA across all 32 layers provides evidence for
implicit translation in reasoning tasks: English-Spanish representational similarity
peaks in mid-layers (Spearman \(r = 0.81\) at layer 8) before declining at the output
stage, consistent with a shared cross-lingual computation resolved in a
language-specific way. Together, our results show that cross-lingual performance
disparities are not merely behavioral but are reflected deep in the residual stream,
and that the degree of cross-lingual representational alignment differs substantially
by task category and language pair.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
%% ============================================================

The rapid deployment of large language models (LLMs) in multilingual settings has
outpaced our understanding of how these models process non-English content
\citep{ahuja2023mega, huang2023not}. While models such as Mistral-7B and Qwen2-7B
are widely used across dozens of languages, they are trained on corpora that are
heavily weighted toward English. This creates an empirical gap: we know behavioral
accuracy often drops for non-English inputs, but the mechanistic story---\emph{why}
and \emph{how} does processing fail, and at what layer?---remains largely unexplored.

A central hypothesis in the field is that instruction-tuned multilingual LLMs engage
in \emph{implicit internal translation}: when given a non-English input, the model
maps it to an internal English-like representation before computing an answer, then
(in theory) projects back to the target language for output
\citep{wendler2024llamas, tang2024language}. This hypothesis has important
implications for fairness and reliability---if a model cannot construct the
intermediate representation for a low-resource language like Basque, it may tokenize
the input successfully yet fail to process it meaningfully.

Our work investigates two complementary questions:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=0pt]
  \item \textbf{Behavioral:} How do accuracy and generation quality vary across English,
  Spanish, and Basque, and across math, factual, and reasoning task categories?
  \item \textbf{Mechanistic:} Do the residual stream representations of semantically
  equivalent questions across languages share relational structure (RSA), and does
  cross-lingual representational similarity arise and decay at predictable layers
  (logit lens)?
\end{enumerate}

We construct a 180-question benchmark of aligned triplets
(English / Spanish / Basque), run two 7B models on GPU via Modal with full hidden-state
extraction at every layer, and present a joint behavioral + mechanistic analysis. Our
key contributions are:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=0pt]
  \item A lightweight multilingual benchmark covering three languages and three task
  categories, with LLM-as-judge evaluation (GPT-4o-mini / Grok-3-mini).
  \item Logit lens trajectories showing that math answers emerge at shallow layers
  independent of language, while Basque factual representations fail to converge even
  by the final layer.
  \item RSA analysis providing evidence for implicit translation in reasoning tasks:
  English-Spanish representations converge in middle layers before diverging at output.
  \item A cross-model comparison showing that Mistral and Qwen2 develop qualitatively
  different internal representations for multilingual content, even when overall
  accuracy is similar.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
%% ============================================================

\paragraph{Multilingual LLM benchmarks.}
Most established benchmarks for LLM evaluation are English-centric, with multilingual
variants often constructed via translation rather than native composition
\citep{hendrycks2020measuring}. Recent efforts such as INCLUDE \citep{include2024}
and MMLU-ProX \citep{mmluprox2025} evaluate LLMs across dozens of languages but focus
on behavioral accuracy rather than internal representations. Our benchmark is smaller
but paired with white-box probing, allowing mechanistic interpretation of behavioral gaps.

\paragraph{Cross-lingual generalization.}
\citet{ahuja2023mega} conduct a large-scale multilingual evaluation across 16 models
and 70 languages, finding consistent degradation for low-resource languages.
\citet{mexa2024} introduce Mexa, measuring alignment between English and non-English
representations in pre-trained models. We build on this direction by analyzing
alignment at every layer of the residual stream and connecting it to task-specific
behavioral accuracy.

\paragraph{Implicit translation hypothesis.}
\citet{wendler2024llamas} provide evidence that LLaMA models internally represent
non-English tokens via English-centric latent spaces using logit lens probing.
\citet{tang2024language} study language-agnostic and language-specific neurons in
multilingual transformers. Our RSA-based analysis provides complementary evidence
that cross-lingual representational structure peaks at intermediate depths,
specifically for reasoning tasks, consistent with a mid-layer implicit translation
stage.

\paragraph{Logit lens and residual stream analysis.}
The logit lens technique \citep{nostalgebraist2020logitlens} projects intermediate
hidden states through the final layer norm and language model head to decode what
the model ``predicts'' at each layer. \citet{din2023jump} apply it to study
factual association retrieval. We extend this approach to a multilingual setting,
comparing ground-truth token rank trajectories across three languages and two models
with different vocabulary sizes.

\paragraph{Representational Similarity Analysis.}
RSA \citep{kriegeskorte2008representational}, originally from computational
neuroscience, has been applied to compare representational geometry across neural
network layers and models \citep{kornblith2019similarity}. We use layer-wise RSA to
measure how much the \emph{relational structure} among questions is shared across
languages---a more nuanced probe than direct vector similarity, since it measures
whether the model treats analogous question pairs as analogously (dis)similar.

\section{Experimental Design}
\label{sec:methods}
%% ============================================================

\subsection{Benchmark Construction}

We constructed a benchmark of 180 questions organized as aligned triplets: each
question appears in English, Spanish, and Basque, sharing the same ground-truth
answer. Questions are split into three categories (20 questions each per language):

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=1pt]
  \item \textbf{Math:} Arithmetic, algebra, and geometry problems. Answers are
  numerals or short symbolic expressions, minimizing lexical variation across languages.
  \item \textbf{Factual:} World knowledge questions covering science, geography, and
  history. Requires retrieval of facts likely represented unevenly across languages
  in the training corpus.
  \item \textbf{Reasoning:} Logic puzzles, lateral thinking, and trick questions.
  Requires structural inference; we hypothesize reasoning structure may be shared
  cross-lingually in intermediate layers.
\end{itemize}

We selected a three-language gradient to span high-resource (English), medium-resource
(Spanish, Indo-European), and low-resource (Basque, language isolate with no close
relatives and minimal training data representation). Original questions were written
in English and translated to Spanish and Basque via GPT-4, with manual verification
to ensure semantic equivalence and avoid translation artifacts.

\subsection{Models}

We evaluate two 7B-parameter instruction-tuned models:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=1pt]
  \item \textbf{Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.2:} 32 transformer layers, $d_\text{model}=4096$,
  vocabulary size 32,000. We use the \texttt{[INST]\allowbreak ...\allowbreak [/INST]} prompt format.
  \item \textbf{Qwen2-7B-Instruct:} 28 transformer layers, $d_\text{model}=3584$,
  vocabulary size 152,064 (a significantly larger multilingual vocabulary). We use
  the Qwen2 chat template.
\end{itemize}

Both models were run on A100 GPUs via Modal in \texttt{float16} with greedy decoding
and \texttt{max\_new\_tokens=50}. We use a single unified model for both black-box
accuracy and all white-box probing, avoiding the cross-model confound present in
our early-phase experiments where we used GPT-3.5-turbo and Gemini for inference but
BLOOM-560M for probing.

\subsection{Black-Box Evaluation}
\label{sec:blackbox}

For each of the 180 questions, we record the model's free-form generation. Answers
are evaluated using an LLM-as-judge framework: we submit each (ground truth, model
answer) pair to GPT-4o-mini (with Grok-3-mini as fallback) with a binary YES/NO
prompt. Results are cached in \texttt{*\_accuracy\_cache.json} to ensure
reproducibility. Accuracy is aggregated by language and category.

\subsection{Logit Lens}
\label{sec:logitlens}

At each of the 32 (Mistral) or 28 (Qwen2) layers, we project the last-token hidden
state $\mathbf{h}^{(\ell)}$ through the final layer normalization and the language
model head $W_{\text{lm}}$, then apply softmax:
\[
  p^{(\ell)} = \text{softmax}(W_{\text{lm}} \cdot \text{LayerNorm}(\mathbf{h}^{(\ell)})).
\]
We record two metrics per question per layer:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=1pt]
  \item \textbf{Ground-truth rank (GT rank):} The rank of the first ground-truth answer
  token in $p^{(\ell)}$ (lower is better; rank 1 means the model already ``points at''
  the correct answer).
  \item \textbf{Top-1 confidence:} $\max_t p^{(\ell)}_t$, indicating how decisive the
  model is at each layer.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Representational Similarity Analysis (RSA)}
\label{sec:rsa}

For each (language, category) group of 20 questions, we extract the last-token
residual stream vector $\mathbf{h}^{(\ell)}_i \in \mathbb{R}^{d_\text{model}}$ at
each layer $\ell$ with \texttt{output\_hidden\_states=True}, skipping the embedding
layer. We then build a $20 \times 20$ Representational Dissimilarity Matrix (RDM):
\[
  \text{RDM}^{(\ell)}_{ij} = 1 - \cos(\mathbf{h}^{(\ell)}_i, \mathbf{h}^{(\ell)}_j).
\]
For each language pair $(\mathcal{L}_1, \mathcal{L}_2)$ and each category, we
compute the Spearman rank correlation between $\text{RDM}^{(\ell)}_{\mathcal{L}_1}$
and $\text{RDM}^{(\ell)}_{\mathcal{L}_2}$ at every layer, yielding a
cross-lingual alignment curve. A high correlation indicates that the model
organizes the relational structure among questions similarly for both languages.

Several patterns stand out. First, \textbf{Basque causes dramatic collapse for

factual accuracy, whereas Basque math achieves 20--65\%. The concrete numerical nature
of math problems provides a partial hedge against language-specific knowledge gaps.

Three key findings emerge from Table~\ref{tab:logitlens} and Figures~\ref{fig:gt_rank}
and~\ref{fig:confidence}:

\paragraph{Finding 1: Math is language-agnostic in early layers.}
Basque math has a \emph{lower} mean GT rank at layer 1 (339) than English (1,160) or
Spanish (1,143), meaning the model already gestures toward the correct answer earlier
in Basque math than in English math. This is counterintuitive given Basque's low
accuracy, but it makes sense: math answers are numerals, which tokenize identically
regardless of surrounding language. The early residual stream quickly identifies
numeric answer tokens based on the question's arithmetic structure alone. By layer 32,
Basque math reaches GT rank 62 with 70.3\% confidence---reasonable, though still
below English (rank 261, 94.2\%) and Spanish (rank 452, 81.0\%).

\paragraph{Finding 2: Basque factual representations fail to converge.}
English factual questions reach a mean GT rank of 26 at layer 32---the model is
essentially pointing directly at the correct answer token. Spanish factual rank (219)
is 8$\times$ worse; Basque factual rank (2,440) is 94$\times$ worse and effectively
random. This catastrophic failure---with the model failing to place the correct answer
within the top 2,440 tokens of the distribution---mirrors the 0\% behavioral accuracy
and confirms that the Basque factual failure is not a generation-time artifact but is
present in the model's internal representation. Notably, Basque factual \emph{confidence}
remains high (84.8\% at L32) despite the catastrophic rank, indicating the model is
``decisive but wrong''---it confidently predicts a token, but not the correct one.

\paragraph{Finding 3: Reasoning confidence reveals ``undecided'' processing.}
Basque reasoning top-1 confidence at layer 32 is only 53.9\%, far below English
(87.0\%) and Spanish (68.8\%). Unlike Basque factual---which is decisive but
wrong---Basque reasoning produces diffuse output distributions, consistent with the
model failing to arrive at any coherent answer. This matches the 5\% behavioral
accuracy.

Table~\ref{tab:rsa} and Figures~\ref{fig:rsa_curves}--\ref{fig:basque_gap} reveal
four findings:

\paragraph{Finding 4: Math has the strongest and most durable cross-lingual alignment.}
EN--ES math RSA begins at $r = 0.906$ at layer 1 and remains at $r = 0.785$ at layer
32---the highest final-layer correlation across all categories and language pairs. Even
EN--EU math maintains a positive correlation (0.389) through all 32 layers, despite
Basque's very different surface form. This indicates that the model internally organizes
math questions by their mathematical structure (the operations, magnitudes, and
expected answer types) rather than by the language they are written in---a form of
language-agnostic representational geometry for arithmetic content.

\paragraph{Finding 5: Basque-English factual alignment becomes structurally random.}
The EN--EU factual RSA drops from 0.508 at layer 1 to $-$0.049 at layer 32,
statistically indistinguishable from zero and occasionally anti-correlated. The
relational structure among Basque factual questions has no correspondence to the
structure among the same questions in English. We interpret this as the model
treating Basque factual content as noise: it tokenizes the input but does not route it
through the factual knowledge retrieval circuits it uses for English. The model's
representation of ``which Basque questions are similar'' has no alignment with which
English questions are similar.

\paragraph{Finding 6: Reasoning EN--ES peaks in mid-layers---evidence for implicit translation.}
The EN--ES reasoning RSA rises from 0.394 at layer 1 to a peak of 0.810 at layer 8,
then declines to 0.558 at layer 32. This non-monotonic mid-layer peak is not present
in math (which is high throughout) or factual (which is high in early-to-mid layers
and stays elevated). The peak-then-decline pattern is consistent with an implicit
translation mechanism: the model first maps both English and Spanish reasoning
problems to a shared internal representation (mid-layers), then resolves the answer
in a language-specific way at the output stage (late layers). The mid-layer peak
implies the model's reasoning machinery is cross-lingual, even though its output
generation is not.

\paragraph{Finding 7: Early layers show language-agnostic structure even for Basque.}
Despite Basque factual representations collapsing by layer 32, the layer-1 RSA for
EN--EU factual (0.508) and EN--EU reasoning (0.303) are moderate---higher than
expected for a near-random language. We attribute this to the earliest transformer
layers extracting surface-level features (BPE subword overlap, positional encodings,
character-level n-grams) that are partially shared across languages before the
model's language-specific processing diverges in middle and final layers.

Table~\ref{tab:rsa_comparison} reveals a striking dissociation: Qwen2 achieves far
better Basque accuracy (37\% vs.\ 8\%), yet Mistral shows consistently \emph{higher}
EN--ES RSA at the final layer across all categories (e.g., math: 0.785 vs.\ 0.470;
reasoning: 0.558 vs.\ 0.196). Simultaneously, Qwen2 shows better EN--EU and ES--EU
RSA for factual and reasoning, consistent with its greater Basque ability.

This dissociation---better Basque accuracy but less English-Spanish alignment---suggests
the two models employ qualitatively different strategies for multilingual processing.
Mistral appears to develop strong cross-lingual representational alignment between
English and Spanish (two similar Indo-European languages) but has nearly no usable
representation for Basque. Qwen2, by contrast, seems to develop a more uniformly
multilingual representational space that provides some handle on Basque but does not
show as strong English-Spanish convergence. This is consistent with Qwen2's training
data including more diverse multilingual content, which may prevent over-specialization
toward the English-Spanish axis.

%% ============================================================
\section{Discussion}
%% ============================================================

\paragraph{The Basque wall.}
Both models show a stark performance discontinuity between Spanish and Basque. For
Mistral, this discontinuity is both behavioral (60\% $\to$ 8\%) and mechanistic
(EN--EU factual RSA 0.782 $\to$ $-$0.049). Basque is a language isolate: it shares
no common ancestor with Indo-European languages, has unique morphological structure,
and is almost certainly underrepresented in both models' training corpora. Our results
suggest that below a critical training data threshold, a model may tokenize a language
without meaningfully processing it---producing confident, wrong answers for factual
questions, or diffuse, uncertain outputs for reasoning.

\paragraph{Math as a language-agnostic domain.}
Across all analyses, math exhibits the weakest language dependence: the best early-layer
GT ranks (Basque math, layer 1), the highest EN--EU RSA at the final layer (0.389 for
Mistral), and the smallest accuracy gap between English and Spanish (45\% vs.\ 50\%
for Mistral). We attribute this to the symbolic nature of mathematical answers: the
model can identify the correct numeric token from the structure of the problem
regardless of which language the problem is posed in, because the answer token itself
is language-invariant. This suggests that tasks with language-invariant answer spaces
may be more robust to multilingual generalization gaps.

\paragraph{The implicit translation hypothesis.}
The RSA mid-layer peak for EN--ES reasoning ($r = 0.394$ at L1, $r = 0.810$ at L8,
$r = 0.558$ at L32) provides the clearest mechanistic evidence for implicit translation
in our data. The interpretation is that the model first constructs distinct per-language
representations (early layers), then converges to a shared reasoning representation
in middle layers---where the structural problem (``who arrives first?'', ``which
number is odd?'') is solved in a language-agnostic way---and finally resolves the
answer token in a language-specific output format. The decline from L8 to L32 reflects
the output-side language specificity, not a failure of cross-lingual understanding.

This finding raises a natural question: why does the mid-layer peak appear for
reasoning but not math? Math does not require this convergence because its answer
tokens are already language-agnostic; the model can output the numeric token directly
from any language's representation. Factual questions, by contrast, depend on knowledge
that is represented in a language-specific way in the training corpus, so the model
cannot converge to a shared intermediate representation---and for Basque, there is no
intermediate representation to converge to at all.

\paragraph{Model architecture and the RSA paradox.}
Qwen2's larger vocabulary (152K tokens) implies a fundamentally different token-level
representation for the same text compared to Mistral (32K tokens). Basque words that
were split into many subwords by Mistral's tokenizer may be represented as single
tokens in Qwen2's larger vocabulary, improving its ability to process Basque inputs.
This tokenization advantage is a plausible explanation for Qwen2's dramatic Basque
accuracy improvement (particularly for math, where the correct answer---a numeral---may
benefit from consistent tokenization). However, the RSA results suggest that this
improved Basque processing does not come via stronger English-Spanish alignment;
rather, Qwen2 may process each language more independently, forgoing the strong
EN--ES cross-lingual structure that Mistral develops.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
Our benchmark is small (20 questions per language-category cell), limiting statistical
power. The LLM-as-judge evaluation may introduce systematic biases, particularly for
Basque, where the judge may have limited ability to evaluate non-English answers.
Basque translations were produced by GPT-4, which may have introduced subtle errors.
We evaluate only two model families at the 7B scale; results may differ for larger
models or models with explicit multilingual training objectives. Finally, our RSA
analysis uses the last-token hidden state, which may not capture all relevant
information in autoregressive transformers.

%% ============================================================
\section{Conclusion}
%% ============================================================

We present a joint behavioral and mechanistic analysis of cross-lingual generalization
in Mistral-7B and Qwen2-7B across English, Spanish, and Basque. Our results show
that cross-lingual performance gaps are not surface-level artifacts but are reflected
in the structure of residual stream representations at every layer. Basque factual
representations become structurally random by the final layer in Mistral, while
math representations maintain positive cross-lingual alignment even for Basque.
Mid-layer RSA peaks for English-Spanish reasoning provide evidence for an implicit
translation stage in the residual stream. Qwen2 is substantially more robust for
Basque but achieves this via different internal representations than Mistral, suggesting
that multilingual robustness can arise through different mechanistic routes.

Our work contributes to the growing literature on multilingual interpretability and
suggests that layer-wise RSA is a useful tool for characterizing \emph{where} and
\emph{how} cross-lingual generalization fails or succeeds. Future directions include
activation patching---substituting English or Spanish residual streams into Basque
forward passes to test whether improved representations improve Basque accuracy---and
scaling to a larger benchmark with statistical power sufficient to isolate individual
question difficulty from language-level trends.
